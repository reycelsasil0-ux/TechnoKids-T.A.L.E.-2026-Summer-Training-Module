Online Research

THE GLOBAL PERSPECTIVE

1. UNESCO: Technology in Education (2023)

What the source says:

UNESCO explains that technology education is essential because digital skills now affect education, communication, employment, and participation in society. The report emphasizes that digital literacy, coding, and responsible technology use are becoming as important as traditional literacy.

Why it resonated with me:

This source resonated with me because it framed technology not simply as a “subject,” but as a modern survival skill. UNESCO’s idea that coding is a “new form of literacy” made me realize that students today are growing up in a world where understanding technology is no longer optional. Just like reading and writing opened opportunities in the past, digital literacy now determines how students will learn, work, and interact in the future.

2. World Economic Forum: Future of Jobs Report 2025

What the source says:

The report identifies AI, digital literacy, data analysis, cybersecurity, and technology-related skills as some of the fastest-growing workforce demands globally. It predicts that millions of future jobs will require technological competencies.

Why it resonated with me:

This report stood out because it connected education directly to employability. It made me think about how schools are not just preparing students to pass exams, but preparing them for careers that may not even exist yet. If students are not exposed to technology education early, they may struggle to compete in the modern workforce.

3. OECD: Digital Education Outlook

What the source says:

OECD highlights how technology education helps students develop 21st-century skills such as critical thinking, collaboration, creativity, and problem-solving. The organization also explains that countries investing in digital education are better preparing students for future societal and economic changes.

Why it resonated with me:

What resonated with me here is the idea that technology education develops more than technical skills. Students learn how to think critically, solve problems creatively, and adapt to change. These are lifelong skills that students need regardless of their future profession.

4. Brookings Institution: British Columbia Computer Science

What the source says:

The study discusses how computational thinking lessons improved students’ planning, problem-solving, and coding abilities. It also highlights how early exposure to computer science can positively affect cognitive development.

Why it resonated with me:

This source resonated with me because it proved that technology education benefits even younger learners. Technology classes are not only about using gadgets; they help students strengthen reasoning, logic, and decision-making skills that are useful in everyday life.

5. UNESCO: World Coding Day Perspective

What the source says:

UNESCO describes coding as a “new form of literacy in the age of artificial intelligence,” emphasizing that coding develops creativity, innovation, and problem-solving abilities.

Why it resonated with me:

This perspective strongly resonated with me because it compares coding to literacy. It reminded me that students today need to understand the language of technology the same way previous generations needed to understand books and written communication.

6. UNESCO: Digital Citizenship Education

What the source says:

The article explains that students must learn how to behave responsibly online, identify misinformation, protect privacy, and participate ethically in digital spaces.

Why it resonated with me:

This source resonated with me because technology education is not only about technical skills—it is also about responsibility. Students today spend a huge part of their lives online, so schools have a responsibility to teach safe and ethical digital behavior.
